# 1. Philosophy

Every screen should answer at least one question about the world.

Examples:

- Who lives here?
- Who built this?
- Why is this abandoned?
- Why is this path worn?
- Why are these trees untouched?

## Rule STORY-001

Every environment must communicate at least one narrative without dialogue.

Severity: Critical

---

# 2. Identity Layers

Every location should have four identities:

1. Functional
2. Visual
3. Emotional
4. Historical

Example:

A mountain village

Functional:
Mining settlement

Visual:
Stone buildings and steep terrain

Emotional:
Safe but isolated

Historical:
Expanded around an old quarry

---

## Rule ID-001

No two neighboring towns should share all four identity layers.

Severity: High

---

# 3. Environmental History

Evidence of time makes worlds believable.

History can be shown through:

- worn paths
- repaired fences
- old foundations
- abandoned structures
- changing vegetation
- erosion
- monuments

## Rule HIST-001

Every important location should imply a past.

---

## Rule HIST-002

Damage should have a visible cause whenever possible.

---

# 4. Biome Identity

Each biome should be recognizable from silhouette alone.

Differentiate using:

- terrain massing
- vegetation
- spacing
- elevation
- landmarks
- color (secondary)

## Rule BIO-001

Do not rely on palette alone to distinguish biomes.

Severity: Critical

---

# 5. Settlement Identity

Every settlement needs a reason to exist.

Possible identities:

- farming
- port
- shrine
- mining
- research
- trade
- military
- tourism

## Rule CITY-001

Architecture, terrain, and layout must reinforce the settlement's purpose.

---

# 6. Human Presence

Players notice traces of people.

Examples:

- gardens
- fences
- bridges
- benches
- cut trees
- maintained roads

## Rule HUMAN-001

Signs of civilization should diminish gradually away from settlements.

---

# 7. Memory & Landmarks

Players build mental maps from memorable objects.

A landmark should be:

- unique
- visible
- stable
- meaningful

## Rule LAND-100

Every major region should contain at least one memorable landmark visible from multiple screens.

---

# 8. Emotional Design

Environment should influence emotion.

| Emotion | Design Tools |
|----------|--------------|
| Safety | Open space, low density |
| Mystery | Occlusion, curves |
| Danger | Compression, high density |
| Wonder | Long sightlines |
| Calm | Repetition and rhythm |
| Isolation | Distance and silence |

## Rule EMO-001

Spatial composition should reinforce intended emotion.

---

# 9. Failure Catalog

SF-001 Generic Town
: Could be swapped with any other town.

SF-002 Decoration Without Meaning
: Props communicate nothing.

SF-003 History Vacuum
: World appears newly created.

SF-004 Biome Clone
: Different areas differ only by palette.

SF-005 Emotional Flatline
: Entire game evokes one feeling.

---

# 10. AI Storytelling Audit

Evaluate:

- What story does this location tell?
- What profession dominates?
- What landmark is remembered?
- What evidence of history exists?
- What emotion is communicated?
- Could this area be confused with another?

Output:

```markdown
# Environmental Story Audit

Identity
History
Emotion
Landmarks

Critical Violations
High Violations
Medium Violations

Only modify spatial storytelling.
Do not increase visual fidelity.
```

---

# World Identity Test

Hide all NPC dialogue.

If a player can still correctly infer:

- what the location is,
- why it exists,
- how people use it,
- and how it differs from neighboring locations,

the environment possesses strong identity.
