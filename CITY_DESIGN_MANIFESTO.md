# Pokémon Map & City Design Manifesto

*A practical design doctrine for building Pokémon-style regions, routes, cities, dungeons, and progression loops.*

---

## Core Thesis

A classic Pokémon map is not an open world. It is a controlled pilgrimage disguised as exploration.

The player feels free because they constantly make small local choices: which patch of grass to enter, which trainer to fight, whether to explore a side path, whether to return to town, whether to push deeper into a cave. But the designer is quietly controlling the order of discovery through roads, gates, one-way ledges, blocked paths, dungeons, badges, HMs, shortcuts, and story events.

A Pokémon region is built from emotional thresholds:

- **Town** = safety, identity, recovery, rumor, preparation.
- **Route** = risk, friction, micro-adventure, transition.
- **Dungeon** = commitment, resource drain, altered navigation.
- **Gym** = judgment, proof, local identity made mechanical.
- **Unlock** = the old world becomes new again.
- **League** = ritualized mastery.

The road is the unit of adventure. The town is the unit of memory. The region is the system that turns movement into growth.

---

## The Prime Directive

> Design the world so that every place has a purpose, every route has a story, every city has a thesis, and every unlock makes the player reinterpret something they saw earlier.

Pokémon maps are not powerful because they are huge. They are powerful because they are legible, compressed, revisited, and emotionally sequenced.

---

# Part I — Regional Structure

## 1. The Region Must Be Legible Before It Is Large

The player should understand the first area quickly. The opening should not overwhelm them with five exits, complex systems, or a sprawling town.

A strong opening town has:

- A home base.
- A mentor or lab equivalent.
- One obvious first exit.
- One visible future route or blocked option.
- Very few buildings.
- A clear reason to leave.
- A feeling of safety before danger.

The first town should be mentally mappable in under 30 seconds.

### Design Rule

Do not begin with scale. Begin with orientation.

The player should think:

> “I know where I am, I know where I came from, and I know where I am supposed to go next.”

---

## 2. The Region Should Be a Network, Not a Scatterplot

A Pokémon region is not random towns connected by filler roads. It is a meaningful travel network.

A convincing region usually contains:

1. Rural origin.
2. First service town.
3. First gym city.
4. Natural barrier.
5. Mid-game hub.
6. Port or travel city.
7. Mood town.
8. Commercial city.
9. Power/institution city.
10. Wilderness/recreation city.
11. Science/industry city.
12. Final ceremonial destination.

Each place should be located where it makes geographic sense:

- Ports belong on water.
- Forests belong near rural edges.
- Mountains divide regions.
- Caves connect or obstruct.
- Commercial centers sit near crossroads.
- Institutional capitals sit near the center.
- Islands require water traversal.
- The League sits at the edge, above, beyond, or behind a gate.

### Design Rule

The map should look like people could actually live there, travel there, trade there, and build institutions there.

---

## 3. The Region Should Fold Back on Itself

A great Pokémon map lets the player return to old places with new abilities, new knowledge, and new goals.

Early areas should contain:

- Water the player cannot cross yet.
- Trees or obstacles the player cannot remove yet.
- Caves the player cannot fully navigate yet.
- NPCs who hint at future destinations.
- Locked buildings.
- Suspicious shortcuts.
- Ledges that show one-way route logic.
- Distant landmarks visible before access.

The player should regularly think:

- “I remember that blocked path.”
- “I saw that water earlier.”
- “That cave exit must connect somewhere.”
- “I can finally open that gate.”
- “This place means something different now.”

### Design Rule

Every early area should contain at least one late-game reason to return.

---

## 4. Progression Should Feel Like Capability, Not Just Permission

Badges, HMs, story events, and items work because they are not merely keys. They change how the player understands the world.

A good unlock does three things:

1. Opens a forward path.
2. Opens at least one old path.
3. Teaches the player a new map verb.

Examples of map verbs:

- Cut.
- Surf.
- Push.
- Fly.
- Light.
- Climb.
- Unlock.
- Cross.
- Enter.
- Return.

### Design Rule

Never give the player an unlock that only opens one door. A good unlock creates a web of remembered possibilities.

---

# Part II — Route Design

## 5. A Route Is a Sentence, Not a Hallway

A route is not empty space between towns. It is a playable sentence with beginning, middle, and end.

A classic route usually contains:

- Safe entrance.
- First encounter zone.
- Trainer pressure.
- Optional item.
- Terrain variation.
- A small choice.
- A landmark.
- A one-way ledge or shortcut.
- Exit compression into the next safe place.

The player should be able to summarize a route in one phrase:

- “The bridge challenge.”
- “The forest maze.”
- “The cave approach.”
- “The coastal walk.”
- “The biker road.”
- “The dangerous sea route.”
- “The final badge gate.”

### Design Rule

Every route needs a micro-story.

Not a cutscene. Not exposition. A spatial story.

---

## 6. Routes Need Controlled Choice

A good Pokémon route rarely gives complete freedom. It gives controlled choice.

Common route choices:

- Walk through grass or around it.
- Fight a trainer or avoid them.
- Grab an item now or return later.
- Take the short dangerous path or long safer path.
- Jump a ledge and commit to forward movement.
- Push deeper before healing or retreat.

The player should make small tactical decisions every few screens.

### Design Rule

If the player is just holding one direction, the route is failing.

---

## 7. Routes Should Teach Through Terrain

The best routes introduce map language without tutorials.

Examples:

- Tall grass teaches risk.
- Ledges teach one-way movement.
- Signs teach orientation.
- Water teaches future access.
- Trees teach blocked paths.
- Caves teach commitment.
- Bridges teach linear challenge.
- Fences teach guided movement.
- Rocks teach puzzle logic.

### Design Rule

A route should teach one main spatial idea at a time.

Do not introduce every terrain type at once. Let the world teach in layers.

---

## 8. Route Difficulty Should Pulse

Pokémon routes work because pressure rises and falls.

A good route rhythm:

1. Leave town safely.
2. See danger before entering it.
3. Fight or avoid a trainer.
4. Find a reward.
5. Face a denser challenge.
6. Discover a shortcut or ledge.
7. Reach safety.

This creates tension without exhaustion.

### Design Rule

Never make every tile equally dangerous. Variation is what creates pacing.

---

# Part III — City Design

## 9. Every City Needs One Dominant Idea

A Pokémon city is not a pile of buildings. It is a thesis.

Each city should answer:

> “What is this place about?”

Examples of city theses:

- Origin.
- Return.
- First judgment.
- Water and bridges.
- Travel and ships.
- Death and memory.
- Commerce and temptation.
- Corporate power.
- Wilderness tourism.
- Science and danger.
- Final ceremony.

Once the thesis is chosen, everything should support it:

- Layout.
- Palette.
- Music.
- NPC dialogue.
- Gym type.
- Landmark.
- Shops.
- Optional rewards.
- Local conflict.
- Route connections.

### Design Rule

Before drawing a city, write this sentence:

> “This city exists to make the player feel ______.”

Then enforce that feeling everywhere.

---

## 10. Cities Are Safe, But Not Empty

A city should relieve pressure without stopping momentum.

Every major city needs:

- Healing/rest point.
- Shop or supply access.
- One major landmark.
- One progression hook.
- One optional reward.
- One rumor or foreshadowing NPC.
- One identity-defining building.
- Clear exits.

The player should be able to do three things in a city:

1. Recover.
2. Learn.
3. Advance.

### Design Rule

Every city needs one mandatory reason to visit and one optional reason to remember it.

---

## 11. City Layout Should Express Function

The layout should reflect what the city is.

Examples:

- A hometown should be compact and simple.
- A port city should pull the eye toward the dock.
- A commercial city should have dense buildings and central shops.
- A corporate city should feel gridlike and institutional.
- A wilderness city should sit near open edges, parks, or safari zones.
- A science island should feel isolated and experimental.
- A final league area should feel ceremonial, not residential.

### Design Rule

Do not place buildings randomly. The city’s shape should reveal its social purpose.

---

## 12. NPCs Are Signposts Wearing Human Skin

Pokémon NPCs do more than add flavor. They teach the map.

NPC functions:

- Explain a local mechanic.
- Hint at a blocked path.
- Foreshadow a future location.
- Reward exploration.
- Add humor or mood.
- Point toward the gym.
- Teach world rules.
- Confirm the city’s identity.

Good NPC dialogue is short, spatial, and useful.

### Design Rule

At least one NPC in every city should tell the player what matters there.

---

# Part IV — Dungeon Design

## 13. Dungeons Are Rhythm Breakers

Routes test travel. Dungeons test commitment.

A dungeon changes the normal rules of movement.

Dungeon types:

- Forest maze.
- Cave network.
- Dark tunnel.
- Tower.
- Corporate building.
- Hideout.
- Mansion/ruin.
- Island cave.
- Victory road.

A dungeon should do at least three things:

1. Drain resources.
2. Change navigation rules.
3. Deliver a unique reward, story beat, or unlock.

### Design Rule

A dungeon should never be generic combat density. It needs a mechanical identity and a narrative reason.

---

## 14. Each Dungeon Needs a Navigation Gimmick

A dungeon should have one dominant navigation idea.

Examples:

- Forest: winding paths and trainer ambushes.
- Cave: ladders, rocks, floor changes.
- Dark tunnel: limited visibility.
- Tower: vertical ascent and mood shift.
- Hideout: switches, elevators, locked doors.
- Mansion: keys, ruined rooms, lore fragments.
- Sea cave: currents, boulders, water puzzles.
- Victory road: combined mastery test.

### Design Rule

The player should remember a dungeon by how it moved, not just what enemies were inside.

---

## 15. Dungeons Need Commitment Points

Before entering a dungeon, the player should understand:

- This may take time.
- I may run out of resources.
- I may need to retreat.
- There is probably something important inside.

Good commitment signals:

- Entrance cave mouth.
- Change in music.
- Darker palette.
- Fewer NPCs.
- No immediate shop.
- Labyrinthine entrance.
- Warning NPC.
- Stronger encounters.

### Design Rule

The dungeon entrance should feel like a threshold.

---

# Part V — Gating, Locks, and Return Loops

## 16. Gates Should Be Visible Before They Are Solvable

The player should see locked affordances before they can use them.

Common Pokémon gates:

- Small trees.
- Water.
- Boulders.
- Dark caves.
- Locked gyms.
- Sleeping blockers.
- Guarded city gates.
- Badge checks.
- One-way ledges.
- Closed buildings.

These make the world feel authored. They turn memory into progress.

### Design Rule

A gate that appears only when the key appears is weak. A gate the player remembers is strong.

---

## 17. Shortcuts Are Emotional Payoffs

A shortcut is not just convenience. It is proof that the world is connected.

Good shortcuts:

- Connect early and mid-game regions.
- Reduce backtracking after a dungeon.
- Reward exploration.
- Make the player feel smart.
- Convert a linear path into a loop.

### Design Rule

Every major dungeon or long route should eventually produce a shortcut, loop, or return path.

---

## 18. The First Area Should Matter Late

The strongest region design returns to the beginning.

Ways to make the first area matter later:

- Final gym in an early city.
- Water route from hometown.
- Locked cave near starter route.
- Late-game NPC reward in first town.
- Rival rematch near home.
- League road visible from early geography.
- New traversal opens old terrain.

### Design Rule

The beginning should not be discarded. It should be recontextualized.

---

# Part VI — Tile Language

## 19. Tiles Are Vocabulary

Pokémon-style maps use simple repeated tiles as a visual language.

Common tile meanings:

- **Trees** = boundary.
- **Tall grass** = encounter risk.
- **Short grass** = safe terrain.
- **Ledges** = one-way movement.
- **Water** = future traversal or sea route.
- **Rocks** = obstruction or puzzle.
- **Fences** = guided movement.
- **Signs** = orientation.
- **Doors** = interaction.
- **Statues** = importance.
- **Flowers** = softness/safety.
- **Dark floors** = danger, grief, cave, ruin, mystery.
- **Bridges** = transition and exposure.
- **Paths** = intended direction.

### Design Rule

The player should understand the function of a tile without reading text.

---

## 20. Constraints Create Style

Classic Pokémon maps are memorable partly because they are constrained.

Useful constraints:

- Limited tile palette.
- Small towns.
- Repeated building shapes.
- Clear cardinal movement.
- Strong silhouettes.
- Few decorative objects per area.
- High contrast between safe and dangerous tiles.
- Landmarks used sparingly.

### Design Rule

Do not solve weak design with more detail. Solve it with clearer structure.

---

# Part VII — City Archetype Blueprint

## 1. Hometown

**Purpose:** Identity and emotional baseline.

Required elements:

- Player home.
- Mentor/lab/training room.
- Starter choice or first major commitment.
- One obvious exit.
- One future blocked route.
- Few NPCs.
- No system overload.

Design feeling:

> “This is where I came from.”

---

## 2. First Service Town

**Purpose:** Teach recovery, supplies, basic systems, and future locks.

Required elements:

- Healing station.
- Shop.
- Tutorial NPCs.
- Locked or unavailable gym/major building.
- Multiple visible exits.
- Hint of later importance.

Design feeling:

> “The world is bigger than home.”

---

## 3. First Badge City

**Purpose:** First proof of competence.

Required elements:

- First gym/challenge.
- Strong local identity.
- Small landmark.
- NPCs explaining type/theme/challenge.
- Nearby dungeon or route pressure.

Design feeling:

> “I am being tested.”

---

## 4. First Hub City

**Purpose:** Make the world feel wider.

Required elements:

- Three or more route connections.
- Side route.
- Optional reward.
- Second major challenge.
- A shop or service with memorable utility.

Design feeling:

> “I have options now.”

---

## 5. Port / Travel City

**Purpose:** Expand the imagined world.

Required elements:

- Dock, vehicle, station, or travel hub.
- Ticket/event gate.
- Gym/challenge.
- Travel-themed NPCs.
- Water boundary.
- Optional item or voucher.

Design feeling:

> “The world extends beyond this region.”

---

## 6. Mood Town

**Purpose:** Tonal rupture.

Required elements:

- Cemetery, tower, ruin, hospital, abandoned facility, or memorial.
- Changed music/palette.
- Fewer cheerful NPCs.
- Story gate.
- Mystery or grief.

Design feeling:

> “Something is wrong here.”

---

## 7. Commercial Metropolis

**Purpose:** Abundance, temptation, optional systems.

Required elements:

- Department store or major shop.
- Game corner/side economy/minigame.
- Dense buildings.
- Optional rewards.
- Villain presence beneath the surface.
- Major gym/challenge.

Design feeling:

> “There is too much to do here.”

---

## 8. Power Metropolis

**Purpose:** Institutional conflict.

Required elements:

- Corporate HQ, university, federation, government, or elite facility.
- Blocked access.
- City-wide crisis.
- Multiple factions.
- Major story battle.
- High-density grid layout.

Design feeling:

> “Power is concentrated here.”

---

## 9. Wilderness / Recreation City

**Purpose:** Alternate encounter rules and exploration rewards.

Required elements:

- Safari zone, park, preserve, camp, arena complex, or special training ground.
- Rare encounters.
- Optional exploration economy.
- Movement unlock.
- Gym/challenge.
- Remote edge-of-map feeling.

Design feeling:

> “This is where the wild system changes.”

---

## 10. Science / Island City

**Purpose:** Late-game mystery, isolation, transformation.

Required elements:

- Lab.
- Ruin/mansion/facility.
- Locked gym/challenge.
- Water access.
- Experimental lore.
- Fossil/ancient/legendary/scientific subplot.

Design feeling:

> “Knowledge here is dangerous.”

---

## 11. League / Final Destination

**Purpose:** Ceremony and mastery.

Required elements:

- Badge/qualification gate.
- Final dungeon.
- Elite architecture.
- Minimal town clutter.
- Healing and final shop only.
- Statues, banners, gates, or ceremonial path.
- No randomness in layout.

Design feeling:

> “I have arrived at the place that judges everything.”

---

# Part VIII — Essential Region Formula

A FireRed-style region can be structured like this:

1. Home village.
2. Route 1 tutorial.
3. First service town.
4. Optional rival side route.
5. Forest/maze dungeon.
6. First gym city.
7. Mountain or cave route.
8. Second hub city.
9. Bridge/test route.
10. Port city.
11. Vehicle or event dungeon.
12. Shortcut back to old region.
13. Dark or hostile dungeon.
14. Mood town.
15. Commercial city.
16. Villain hideout.
17. Long southern/eastern route chain.
18. Safari/wilderness city.
19. Major city crisis.
20. Water traversal unlock.
21. Sea route.
22. Island/science city.
23. Return to first city for final badge.
24. Badge gate.
25. Victory dungeon.
26. League.

This structure works because it repeatedly changes what the player thinks the world is:

- First, the world is a road north.
- Then it is a chain of towns.
- Then it is a loop.
- Then it is a network.
- Then it is a world full of locked memories.
- Then it becomes a final pilgrimage.

---

# Part IX — Non-Negotiable Design Rules

## The Map Fails If It Lacks These

- A safe-risk-safe rhythm.
- Routes with identity, not filler.
- Cities with one dominant thesis.
- Early locked objects that later become meaningful.
- Shortcuts that reward memory.
- Optional areas that feel discovered, not dumped.
- Dungeons that change navigation rules.
- Landmarks visible before they are useful.
- NPCs who explain world logic through gossip.
- A final approach that ritualizes mastery.

---

## The Map Should Constantly Ask These Questions

For every town:

- What emotion defines this place?
- What does the player need here?
- What does the player learn here?
- Why will the player remember it?
- How many exits does it have?
- What future thing is visible but inaccessible?

For every route:

- What is the micro-story?
- What terrain idea does it teach?
- What choices does it offer?
- What pressure does it create?
- What reward does it hide?
- What landmark makes it memorable?

For every dungeon:

- Why must the player enter?
- What navigation rule changes?
- What resource pressure exists?
- What reward or story beat waits inside?
- How does the player exit or loop back?

For every unlock:

- What forward path opens?
- What old path opens?
- What remembered obstacle becomes meaningful?
- What new verb has the player gained?

# Part X — Applying This to a Wrestling RPG

Do not build “Pokémon but wrestling” as a literal reskin. Build the same spatial grammar.

| Pokémon Element | Wrestling RPG Equivalent |

| Poké Mart | Gear shop, supplement stand, tape station |
| Gym | Dual meet, club challenge, regional room, tournament final |

## Wrestling Region Structure

A wrestling RPG region could follow this pilgrimage:

1. **Hometown Wrestling Room**  
   Small, safe, personal. Introduces identity and first partner.

2. **Local Neighborhood Route**  
   Basic encounters: youth wrestlers, club kids, first drills.

3. **First Town Dual**  
   Teaches recovery, gear, basic challenge format.

4. **School Gymnasium Dungeon**  
   Bleachers, locker rooms, mats, first bracket.

5. **First Badge Equivalent: Local Tournament Title**  
   The first proof that the player belongs.

6. **College Campus Hub**  
   Multiple exits, training options, recruiting pressure, optional side quests.

7. **Rival Club Facility**  
   Stronger opponents, hostile environment, unique training reward.

8. **Regional Qualifier City**  
   Bigger stakes, seeding, weigh-ins, bracket logic.

9. **Commercial Sports District**  
   Gear, NIL, sponsorships, side systems.

10. **Mood Town Equivalent: Injury / Weight-Cut Arc**  
   Tonal rupture. Recovery, doubt, discipline, cost.

11. **National Training Center**  
   Elite access, advanced techniques, institutional power.

12. **Airport / Bus Route / Travel Unlock**  
   Opens distant events and revisits old areas.

13. **Major Rival University**  
   City-wide challenge, facility comparison, recruiting conflict.

14. **Conference Championship**  
   Qualification gate, high-level bracket, story battle.

15. **Final Arena Approach**  
   Credentials, media hallway, weigh-in room, warm-up tunnel.

16. **National Tournament / Trials**  
   Ceremonial final test.

## Wrestling City Archetypes

### Hometown Room

Feeling: humble origin.  
Function: identity, first partner, first coach, first exit.

### Local Tournament Town

Feeling: first real test.  
Function: brackets, gear table, small gym, first title.

### College Campus City

Feeling: opportunity and pressure.  
Function: training facilities, recruiting, multiple routes, team hierarchy.

Feeling: comparison and insecurity.

Feeling: temptation.

Feeling: ceremony.

# Part XI — Practical Production Rules

## Build Order

Do not build the whole map at once.

Build in this order:

1. One hometown.
2. One Route 1.
3. One first service town.
4. One first dungeon.
5. One first boss/gym.
6. One shortcut back.
7. One locked future object.
8. One optional side path.
9. One second city with a stronger thesis.
10. Only then expand the region.

## Minimum Viable Pokémon-Style Map

A small but satisfying vertical slice needs:

- 2 towns.
- 2 routes.
- 1 dungeon.
- 1 boss/gym.
- 1 locked future path.
- 1 shortcut.
- 1 optional item path.
- 1 visible future landmark.
- 1 NPC who foreshadows the next region.

If those are strong, the game will feel more real than a huge empty map.

# Part XII — The Manifesto Condensed

1. The map is a pilgrimage, not a playground.
2. Roads are adventures, not connectors.
3. Towns are memories, not service menus.
4. Every city needs one thesis.
5. Every route needs one micro-story.
6. Every dungeon needs one navigation rule.
7. Every unlock must recontextualize old terrain.
8. Gates must be seen before they are solved.
9. Shortcuts are emotional rewards.
10. The first area must matter late.
11. Tile language must be instantly readable.
12. Constraint is not a weakness; it is the source of clarity.
13. The final approach must feel ceremonial.
14. The region should feel lived-in, not assembled.
15. The player should always know where they are, wonder what is beyond, and remember what they could not reach before.
